Reservoir Storage

Drought reduces snowpack and results in lower reservoir storage capacity.

Over 80% of public drinking water supply comes from rivers, lakes, reservoirs, and other surface waters (1).

Here, the effect of drought on 59 of California's >700 reservoirs are shown through time.

Reservoir Capacity (%)
California reservoirs sorted by elevation

Reservoir volume as a percent of total volume is shown for 2011-2014. The width of bars is proportional to reservoir maximum capacity, so that bigger reservoirs have larger bars. Reservoirs are ordered from low elevation (left) to high elevation (right). Average reservoir volume decreases as the California drought persists.

Drought, snowpack, and reservoir storage

Snowpack in California is the primary source of water to reservoirs that serve drinking water, agriculture, and hydroelectric needs.

For any given year, less wintertime snowpack results in lower reservoir levels, and less water for consumptive use.

In 2014, California has received only 20% of its normal precipitation and had only 18% of its normal snow pack, and has faced a devastating three years of drought (2). Right now, 100% of California faces severe drought conditions (3).

Drought and Food Prices

Agriculture represents the largest fraction of freshwater withdrawals(1).

For the first time in history, some agricultural areas are not receiving any water for irrigation which is resulting in devastation of historically productive areas. The agricultural output for the state could fall by $3.5 billion this year (3).
